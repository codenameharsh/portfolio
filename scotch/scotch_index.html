Case Study:

Scotch & Soda

Prermium Retail Strategy Visual Merchandising Fashion Consultant

PROJECT OVERVIEW

During my internship at Scotch & Soda, Chennai, I worked as a Fashion Consultant Intern. My role was positioned at the intersection of consumer psychology and visual aesthetics.
I was tasked with maintaining the integrity of a premium Dutch label while translating its "Amsterdams Blauw" and "Maison Scotch" narratives for a discerning Indian clientele.

  • Role: Fashion Consultant
  • The Mission: To translate Scotch & Soda’s quirky, layered Dutch aesthetic into a relatable experience for the Chennai market.

DESIGN PILLARS


01

Curated Eclecticism

Mixing prints, textures, and eras to create a "collected" rather than "bought" look.

02

Customer-Centric Storytelling

Using visual cues to guide the customer through a narrative journey within the store.

03

Strategic Functionality

Balancing global high-fashion standards with the operational realities of a fast-paced retail environment.

VISUAL DNA

  • The Scotch & Soda aesthetic is rooted in the "Wardrobe Style"—a display logic that mimics how a person actually dresses.
  • The palette focuses on harmonious color stories, limiting each rack to four distinct shades or tints to ensure a premium, uncluttered experience.

TECH STACK

  • Retail Ops: SAP (ERP for inventory and billing).
  • E-Commerce Integration: Omni-channel fulfillment for Tata Cliq Luxury and Ajio Luxe.
  • CRM & Marketing: Conversant (One-to-one digital communication tools).
  • Analysis: Daily Sales Reports (DSR) and KPI tracking (UPT, Conversion, ATV).

Challenge

The challenge was to drive high Units Per Transaction (UPT) during a seasonal transition while the physical product mix (heavy knits vs. light linens) was in flux.

THE PROBLEM

How do you maintain the brand’s global "Amsterdam-cool" identity, which relies heavily on layering and heavy textures in a tropical, humid climate like Chennai?

CORE COMPONENTS & LOGIC

Strategic Retailing

In a premium retail environment, the goal is to shift the customer’s mindset from "buying an item" to "investing in a look." I focused on two core pillars to drive this: the Wardrobe Style Visual Merchandising and the Silent Sale Strategy.

01

The Wardrobe Style VM

Rather than adhering to traditional category-based merchandising where all shirts or all trousers are grouped together, I implemented the "Wardrobe Style" display. This logic is rooted in the natural psychology of how a person gets undressed.

  • The Sequential Flow: Racks were organized in a specific descending order: Coats → Pullovers → Shirts → Tees → Bottoms.
  • The Logic:
    By seeing the layers together in a logical sequence, the customer is encouraged to mentally "trial" a complete outfit.
    It reduces decision fatigue and naturally facilitates cross-selling, as the next logical piece of the outfit is always within arm's reach.

02

The Silent Sale Strategy

Premium brands face a unique challenge during discount seasons: how to move inventory without diluting the brand’s luxury perception. To address this, I helped execute the "Silent Sale."

  • One-to-One Communication:
    Instead of aggressive "SALE" banners and loud store windows, we utilized Conversant’s CRM Media. We engaged in highly personalized outreach via WhatsApp and direct calls to our existing VIP database.
  • The Logic:
    By keeping the sale "silent" to the general public and exclusive to the loyalist, we maintained the brand's premium positioning. We used data-driven insights to reach out to potential customers with items tailored to their past purchase history.

DESIGN PROCESS

My process followed a structured retail design cycle:

  1. Observation: Analyzing footfall patterns to identify "cold zones" in the store.
  2. Hypothesis: If we move the high-margin "Amsterdam Couture" collection to the front-right (the natural path of a customer), conversion will increase.
  3. Execution: Re-merchandising the stand-alone walls and "Amsterdams Blauw" denim wall using an accent-to-light color gradient.
  4. Iteration: Reviewing the DSR at the end of the day to see if the restructured rack led to more "look" sales vs. single-item sales.

The Refinement Arc

The most challenging part was the transition from AW19 (Collectors Atelier) to SS20 (Painting Paradise). We faced a backlog of winter stock during a heatwave. I had to pivot the styling logic, using heavy jackets as "shoulder-drapes" on mannequins rather than core pieces, ensuring the store looked seasonally relevant without ignoring the inventory we needed to move.

CHALLENGES AND TAKEAWAYS

THE HURDLES

  • The "Discount" Perception:
    Maintaining a premium brand image during the End of Season Sale (EOSS). We solved this by gamifying the experience—using "Christmas Stockings" for discount reveals rather than aggressive red "Sale" stickers.
  • Logistical Friction:
    Managing the flow of goods from the warehouse to the shop floor while ensuring the "Freshness" of the display was never compromised.

THE INSIGHTS

  • Color Theory as a Sales Tool:
    I learned that limiting a rack to 4 shades isn't just an aesthetic choice; it reduces "decision fatigue" for the customer, leading to faster conversions.
  • Data-Driven Styling:
    I learned that fashion isn't just about what looks good; it’s about what the data dictates. Styling became a tool to fix "slow movers" identified in the morning DSR.

Scope & Future Improvements

  • Hyper-Localization:
    Exploring AI-driven inventory that predicts local needs (e.g., specific Chennai festival seasons) rather than strictly following the European calendar.
