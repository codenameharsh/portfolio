Case Study:

Yonderlust

User Interface Design Mobile App 48-hr Sprint

PROJECT OVERVIEW

YonderLust is a high-fidelity mobile solution designed to bridge the gap between the thrill of solo exploration and the practical need for safety.

  • Role: Solo UX/UI Designer
  • Duration: 48-Hour Sprint (2026)
  • The Mission: Transform complex travel logistics into a simplified, visual journey of self-discovery.

DESIGN PILLARS


01

Emotional Safety

Interfaces that feel "supportive" rather than technical

02

Map-Centricity

Constant orientation to eliminate the fear of getting lost.

03

Modular Planning

A "low-cognitive-load" card system for on-the-go decisions.

VISUAL DNA

  • Logo: A walking shoe sketch representing adventure and personal journeys.
  • Tagline: "Explore Yourself." — Encouraging self-discovery through travel.
  • Color Palette: Soft teal and charcoal for a balance of calm, trust, and excitement.

TECH STACK

Proposed Build: React Native (Cross-platform fluidity), Mapbox SDK (Customized spatial UI), and Gemini API (Contextual dashboard logic).

Challenge

Solo travel offers total freedom but triggers deep anxieties regarding safety and isolation.
Most travel apps dump data on users, leading to "Decision Paralysis."
My goal: transition a user from hyper-vigilance to empowerment in 48 hours.

THE PROBLEM

“How might we empower solo travelers to explore confidently while feeling safe, supported, and inspired?”

key features

CORE COMPONENTS & LOGIC

01

VISUAL STORYBOARD

A drag-and-drop itinerary builder that functions like a mood board.

02

CONTEXTUAL DASHBOARD

Surfacing "Nearby Gems" based on time of day (e.g., well-lit events at night).

03

THE DISCREET SOS

A fluid architecture that prioritizes thumb-friendly targets and legible type on any screen.

04

INTERACTIVE MAP

Live pins for places, events, cafes, and parks. Offers spatial awareness at a glance.

DESIGN PROCESS

Using Digital Ethnography, I mined r/solotravel to find the "hidden" pain points. I skipped traditional wireframing to move straight into "High-Fidelity Iteration", designing directly with my final palette to test the emotional impact of the colors immediately.

The Messy Middle

The hardest pivot was the Safety UI. Initially, the SOS features felt "alarmist." Through iteration, I moved away from "Security Tool" aesthetics toward "Compassionate Guide" aesthetics, softening the UI while keeping the functionality accessible.

CHALLENGES AND TAKEAWAYS

THE HURDLES

  • The "Safety vs. Scary" Balance:
    I spent a lot of time wrestling with the map. I wanted the user to feel safe, but I didn’t want the interface to look like a crime-watch app.
    Finding the sweet spot where safety data felt like "helpful context" rather than "constant danger" was my biggest IA challenge.
  • The 48-Hour Filter:
    In a sprint this fast, you can’t build everything. I had to kill some "cool" features to make sure the core "High-Energy" flow (the moments when a traveler is actually out and about) was flawless. It was a lesson in brutal prioritization.

THE INSIGHTS

  • Constraints aren't just obstacles; they’re a clarity filter.
    Because I didn’t have time for "feature bloat," I was forced to stop obsessing over buttons and start obsessing over the user's pulse. I realized that a designer's job isn't just to organize information; it's to manage the user's stress levels.

Scope & Future Improvements

  • The "Vibe Check" (AI Recharge):
    I want to build a mode that asks, "How’s your social battery?"
    If you’re drained, the app pivots to quiet libraries and solo-friendly parks; if you’re buzzing, it finds the crowds.
  • Accountability Buddies:
    Not a dating app, but a "safety-first" social layer.
    A way to find someone to grab coffee with just so someone knows where you are, without the friction of a formal meetup.
