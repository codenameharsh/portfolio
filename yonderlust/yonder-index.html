YonderLust: Explore Yourself

Case Study | Solo Travel App UI Design

Duration: 2 days

Overview

YonderLust is a conceptual solo travel app designed during a two-day design sprint. The idea stemmed from a personal fascination with solo travel—not just as a physical journey, but as an act of self-exploration. I wanted to design something that could make that journey feel more approachable, especially for those who crave independence but feel daunted by the unknown.

The goal? To create an experience that reassures, empowers, and encourages users to take the leap into solo adventures—with both excitement and peace of mind.

Project Goals

  • Solve core challenges faced by solo travelers: safety, planning fatigue, and loneliness.
  • Prioritize emotional design—make the app feel warm, trustworthy, and intuitive.
  • Build a high-fidelity prototype that communicates the core product experience.
  • Apply a full UX workflow under time constraints—from research to wireframes to polished UI.

Research & Learning Process

    Although I didn’t have time for formal user interviews, I drew insights from:
  • Reddit solo travel threads
  • Travel blog comments
  • App reviews on Google Play and the App Store
    Common pain points emerged:
  1. “I want to travel alone but I’m scared I’ll get lost or feel unsafe.”
  2. “Group tours don’t appeal to me, but it’s hard to plan on my own.”
  3. “I love going solo but wish there was a way to meet like-minded people on the road.”

These quotes stuck with me. I realized my job wasn’t just to design an app, it was to design trust, emotional safety, and intuitive freedom.

I also mapped out the emotional journey of a solo traveler: from initial inspiration to anxiety to curiosity to confidence. This curve influenced my interface decisions: when to give users breathing room, when to offer hand-holding, and when to celebrate their independence.

Target Audience

  • Solo travelers (ages 18–40)
  • Students, digital nomads, explorers
  • Travelers who seek independence but also safety and guidance

Design Process

  1. Problem Definition:

    Solo travelers are often caught between the thrill of freedom and the fear of being unsupported. I defined the core problem as:

    How might we empower solo travelers to explore confidently while feeling safe, supported, and inspired
  2. Ideation:

    Starting with post-it sketches and mind maps, I focused on core pillars:

    • Discover: Personalized local recommendations
    • Plan: Stress-free visual itinerary building
    • Protect: Embedded safety tools without being intrusive
    • Feel: A calming and inviting visual language

    3. I also asked myself:

    • What doesn’t a solo traveler want? (Clutter, info overload, complex flows)
    • What does a solo traveler need? (Reassurance, clarity, connection)
  3. Wireframes:

    I began with low-fidelity wireframes to block out flows before focusing on aesthetics. I tested layout logic through basic paper sketches, then moved into grayscale Figma frames. Key ideas I validated:

    • A map-centric homepage: for spatial orientation and a sense of control.
    • Three-tab flow: Home, Explore, Events (lightweight and familiar)
    • Map pins for nearby events and points of interest.
    • Safety CTA always visible: but non-intrusive

    I opted for a modular card system to surface different types of information (events, spots, saved itineraries) while keeping content digestible and swipeable on the go.

  4. High-Fidelity Prototype:

    This is where the emotional tone took shape.

    • Color palette: I chose a calming teal (symbolizing safety and trust) paired with charcoal for readability and warmth.
    • Typography & icons: Rounded, soft edges to counteract the sharpness of travel anxiety.
    • Imagery: Curated scenes that spark imagination—a rooftop view, a bustling market, a serene lake, because images are the first thing that stirs a traveler’s heart.
    • Logo: A minimalist walking shoe sketch—a metaphor for personal journeys, one step at a time.

Key Features

  1. Personalized Home Dashboard:

    Shows local spots, curated events, and safety quick links—all based on user location and interests.

  2. Explore Nearby with Maps:

    Live pins for places, events, cafes, and parks. Offers spatial awareness at a glance.

  3. Visual Itinerary Builder

    Plan your trip like a storyboard. Drag, drop, edit—and see your day unfold clearly.

  4. Safety First

    Discreet yet accessible SOS button, live location sharing, and emergency contact setup.

  5. Intuitive UI

    Designed for mobility: big buttons, thumb-friendly cards, and minimal screens to reduce friction.

Tools Used

  • Figma: UI design + prototyping
  • Illustrator: For icons and vector elements
  • Photoshop: Image prep
  • Google Maps API: Mock integration for map features

Final Outcome & Impact

By the end of the sprint, I had created a high-fidelity Figma prototype, telling the story of YonderLust through:

  • A gentle, emotionally aware interface
  • Clear user flows that reduce anxiety
  • A travel experience that feels personal and empowering

While not built for production, the design stands as a proof of concept—one that blends utility with emotion.

Final Thoughts

    Key Takeaways
  • Designing for solo travelers is as much about emotional design as it is about functionality.
  • Even under time pressure, staying grounded in user emotions and mental models leads to stronger decisions.
  • The simplest UI choices (like persistent maps or soft shadows) can dramatically improve user comfort.
    Next Iterations I’d Explore:
  • Smart suggestions based on behavioral data and real-time context.
  • A “Connect with fellow travelers” feature that allows opt-in anonymous meetups.
  • Community safety ratings and peer-generated insights for each location or event.

Why YonderLust? Because I’ve always believed solo travel is one of the most powerful ways to discover who you are. This project let me design not just for solo journeys, but for self-discovery, courage, and joy.
